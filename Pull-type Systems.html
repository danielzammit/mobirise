1. The Distinction Between Push and Pull Production Systems.

In general, there are two main methods as to how goods are moved through a production line. These are the Push Production method and Pull Production method. A comparison of which is shown in the figure below.

In Push Production, a workpiece which has just completed a process at a specific station is immediately sent on to the next station, irrespective as to the ‘readiness’ of the receiving station. If the receiving station is not able to process the workpiece immediately a work-in-progress (WIP) pile soon develops.

On the other hand, pull production systems operate as their name suggests, they pull work through the production line to meet customer demands. 

There are a number of ways in which pull systems are applied and these are dependent on the characteristics of the particular manufacturing systems - for example, whether production is carried out ‘to stock’ or ‘to order’. This production type and others account for the variabilities in the implementation of pull-type systems.

2. The Pull-type Production System.

Typically however, pull systems operate as shown in the figure on the left, below. In this example material passes through the flow line from stations 1 to 2 to 3 and finally to 4. 

When a demand is placed, either by a customer or by a subsequent station, it is fulfilled by station 4. When station 4 is scarce of the required components, a signal is passed on to the preceding station i.e. station 3 in this case. Station 3 will then supply the required material to the next station (station 4) and, if necessary, or if material is also scarce in station 3, will pass a signal to its preceding station for additional material. This process is repeated all along the production line. 

An important point of consideration in pull production systems is that machines and/or operations do not produce an item or component unless it is required by a subsequent station or operation. This follows on from the philosophy that there is no necessity for an item to be produced unless it is specifically required in a subsequent operation and which would otherwise be categorised as work-in-progress while unnecessarily occupying factory floor space. In contrast to this, the traditional approach has been for a machine or operation to be run continuously at maximum speed, regardless as to whether subsequent machines or operations actually require the material or component being processed, or are able to handle the output of the preceding machine or operation. The result of this would often be greater manufacturing lead times and work-in-progress levels, and which might also contribute to clutter and confusion on the shop floor. 

Pull-type systems are also helpful in identifying problems along the production line. One of the features of the pull system philosophy is the the system should have control signals which flag issues such as bottlenecking and excessive lead times. Suppose, for example, that station 3 in the production line of the figure to the right is a temporary bottleneck. This means that station 3 is not processing as much material/components as stations 1 and 2. Therefore, stations 1 and 2 are idle for most of the time making it clearly visible to the line supervisor that a problem is present and requires attention. 

One other advantage of pull-type systems is the ability of the system to gradually reduce work-in-progress levels and manufacturing lead times. Consider a system whereby batches of items or components are carried from station to station using colour-coded carts. In front of each station a queue of filled carts is kept waiting to be processed. When a stations uses all the items or components in the cart, the empty cart is sent back to the previous station. This previous station fulfills its task, in doing so emptying its next cart, and fills the previously mentioned cart with processed items, and is returned to its original station. The figure directly above shows the process just described. 

By having this buffer queue in front of each station, work-in-progress levels and lead times may be reduced. In the example above, the number of carts in front of a station can be gradually reduced until the bare minimum number of carts are in front of a particular station at any given time without causing any problems.

3. Limitations with Pull-type Production Systems.

The system and philosophy behind pull-type systems, i.e. that in which information flows backwards through the production line may be of a disadvantage when encountering long lead times.

Consider the example in the figure to the right, where lead times for stations 1, 3, and 4 are 1 day each while station 2 has a lead time of 17 days. In this situation it would take a control signal 18 days to go from station 4 to station 1. This is not ideal and would cause major problems if demand at the final station changes drastically.

In this case, an MRP system is used to hasten the feedback to the preceding stations. The MRP system therefore notifies stations preceding the station having longer lead times of any changes in demand, effectively bypassing the pull control signal. In the example of the figure to the left, station 1 is receiving the required information directly from the MRP system and not from station 2. Through the use of MRP, the system is said to be operating as a combined push-pull system.
