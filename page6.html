1. Introduction to the Just-in-Time (JIT) Principle.

The main goal of every manufacturing company is to optimise its processes to create high quality products within a reasonable time frame, whilst preserving profits wherever possible. A common method of process optimisation is through the implementation of Kanbans, which falls under the umbrella of JIT manufacturing.

The use of such systems is highly regarded in its effectiveness in reducing inventory. Inventory in itself is an inherent burden to the manufacturing company as it incurs additional costs in the form of interest payments, maintenance, boarded surpluses and price-cutting. Aside from creating additional costs, excess inventory takes up storage space, consumes extra energy, and requires further management to be correctly organised. The incidence of excess inventory generally stems from unbalanced capacity and inventory flow, monthly rushes, ‘just-in-case’ inventory and outdated inventory flow methods. In summary, though the implementation a JIT manufacturing method, a company may optimise its pull-processes and create more efficient material flow through the use of Kanbans [1],[2].

Software tools can be used to speed up the paper-lead time. However, this might not be the case in the physical-lead time phase of the manufacturing process. Instead, changes are often necessary on the factory floor so that it suits the modern requirements and is aligned to the JIT philosophy . These changes are known as ‘JIT Improvements’.
The JIT production is a manufacturing approach in which the customer’s requirements are put before and above everything else.
JIT refers to the timing of production flow; goods are delivered to the manufacturing lines just in time to be used, just in the immediately needed quantities, and just to the production processes that need them. Saying “in time” is not enough, since parts can arrive at processes a week or two prior to their use and still be there “in time.”
JIT is defined as a processing method in which products are supplied at a regularised pace relative to other push processes, where the receiving stations can make use of such supplies as soon as they are required. In other words, the mother company must make sure that their customers and stations receive their products ‘just-in-time’.

In this way, the amount of excess accumulated inventory is reduced massively, since the company now has the ability to adapt its production with respect to the consumption of downstream processes. In JIT the ‘in-time’ does not directly imply that products arrive just before they are required, however they arrive within a time period which is suitable for comfortable consumption. The following is a list of improvements often carried out in manufacturing system in order to fit the JIT philosophy:

Flow manufacturing

The aim is to have each product follow the schedule prepared beforehand, and keep the same rhythm throughout the system.

Multi-process handling

in the circumstances where a number of similar operations need to be done, these can be assisted by one or a small group of labourers, hence reducing the human resources required.

Manpower reduction

in JIT working system the goal is to have the least possible number of labourers for a certain production type.

Visual control

often a hands-on approach is the way to go so as to address some everyday issues and resolved in a fast manner.

Levelling

it is important that the production amounts and the workforce in JIT are balanced throughout the company, so as to keep a smooth production rate.

Changeover

the objective of changeover is to reduce the time period necessary for works namely replacement of dies, blades, etc.

Quality assurance

this necessitates an all-round where all the factors that have an effect on the product are checked upon, be it people, goods, production equipment, and production methods.

Standardised operations

this simply means that when certain methods of production have proven to be successful, it is equally important that they are preserved and kept within the manufacturing system.

Jidoka

Human automation: the nature of humans is imposed on the manufacturing system, especially where flexibility and modifications are needed

Maintenance and safety

safety is needless to say put above everything else in JIT, so as to prevent stoppages in the working life of the company.

JIT moves forward and evolves by pure innovation, a different mentality with respect to classic production management, given that in classic production management little or no changes are done leading to minor improvements. Thus, it is more fitting to call it ‘JIT innovation’ rather than ‘JIT improvement’ [2].

2. The JIT Production System.

The production system involves a number of necessary steps in order to sustain its efficiency. Hereunder, are a list of steps and points which must be taken in consideration.

1

Set-up an improvement promotion office
The company sets a small room in a relatively quiet area of the working site. This venue is designed with the purpose of having discussions so that the daily procedures can be improved.

2

Have at least one “improvement day” a week
It is very important for a group of people to set a clear date and location of holding a meeting, as this avoids the occurrence of missing the meeting, or not keeping one regularly.

3

Improvement meetings: one hour is better than two hours
Focus should be kept on quality over quantity. Agenda’s should be strictly adhered to,
and work on possible solutions should be started immediately.

4

Create an improvement list
An improvement list is very similar to a to-do list, and which should be handed to the members in the system at the end of the meeting. This should be the objective of the meeting, that at the end people list with an improvement list.

5

Improvements happen in the factory
Finally, if all the other points are executed correctly, or at the best level possible, then
small improvements in the system can be easily identifiable and resolvable [3].

3. Overview of Lean Manufacturing
The core idea of lean manufacturing is actually quite simple, that is to relentlessly work on eliminating waste from the manufacturing process. Waste is defined as any activity that does not add value from the customer’s perspective. In essence, the main point of working with lean manufacturing is to reduce the amount of wasteful processes created within a factory. In other words, lean manufacturing is a method of producing high quality goods with the least possible residue [3].

Seven Deadly Wastes of Lean Manufacturing

1. Overproduction​: manufacturing an amount of product more than which is necessary. This comes with the risk of the extra products remaining unsold.
Countermeasures: Pace production, pull system, reduction in setup times.

2. Waiting​: time-wasting is a serious waste which can prove to be economically costly to any business.
Countermeasures: continuous flow, standardized work, effective communication.

3. Transport​: Unnecessary movement of materials from one location to another. Countermeasures: Value stream mapping, continuous flow, theory of constraints.

4. Motion​: Unnecessary movement of labourers from one location to another.
 Countermeasures: logically organized, arrangement of equipment to reduce motion.

5. Overprocessing​: processes which the product can do without, costing time and
money.
Countermeasures: compare customer requirements to manufacturing specifications, highlighting potential simplifications to the manufacturing process

6. Inventory​: importing of goods that include accessories which are not required. They are therefore considered a surplus to requirement.
Countermeasure: raw materials are brought in as necessity. This reduces the number of steps during manufacturing.

7. Defects​: after quality checks, extra work is done on the workpiece
Countermeasures: better manufacturing processes and design processes so as to inform the user about any issues which the goods may suffer from and settle them. Common mistakes and noted so that these can be in avoided in future operations [3].

Giving Context to JIT Manufacturing

Just-in-time manufacturing is a type a process in which inventories are minimized in such a way that the exact number of units are produced in order for the next process to initiate. This process was first developed by Taiichie Ohno for Toyota Motors in Japan. Their objective was to eliminate any works in process or inventories as much as possible. In a JIT philosophy, any build-up of products at a station are seen as a burden to the company as they take up space, money, investment funds, and workers [1],[2]. The process was always client oriented such that any fluctuations in demand will be levelled by adjustments in the production flow.


Just-in-time refers to the way that products are delivered to the manufacturing lines. In this case they must always arrive just as they are about to be used or are required. A JIT system must also produce exactly the right number of components that are needed to fulfil the next stage/operation, and is supplied right when it is needed, thus the components are ‘just in time’ [1],[2]. JIT processes are the most effective in high volume and repetitive manufacturing systems, such as in the automobile industry. In such an industry the potential for in-process inventory accumulation is high, since the flow 

quantities of products and components are both high. The ideal batch size here is one part, known as one-piece flow. Thus, batch size must be kept to a minimum. However, producing too many units may be equally problematic as producing too few in markets which experience sharp fluctuations [1],[4]. Naturally, for a JIT system to be able to operate, a pull system of production is required. This is due to fact that communication between downstream and upstream processes is vital for the correct regulation of material flow. Kanbans were developed for this purpose and are still widely utilised today.

Advantages of Implementing JIT

To look at the advantages of implementing JIT one has to look at the advantages of not having a large inventory. High inventory requires labour costs where staff needs to control and manage the inventory. Such labour costs aren’t enhancing the value of the product and are therefore a non-value-adding activity. Hence, they are an added expense which are lowering the profit margin for the company and which are being drastically decreased through the use of JIT manufacturing. Another advantage is that one has more space since less inventory is being stored. Having a small inventory leads to more accuracy when it comes to calculations with regard inventory storage. (Less items to store results in less mistakes such as counting errors, transaction errors, etc.) With high inventories, quality problems in components being stored can go undetected, thus when having a smaller storage such damaged goods can be noticed easier. Having large inventory hides a lot of defects during the manufacturing process. Small inventory exposes such defects and enables companies to increase the quality of their products [1].
